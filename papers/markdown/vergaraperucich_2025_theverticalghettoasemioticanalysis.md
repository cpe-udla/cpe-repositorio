Abstract
This article examines vertical ghettos in Santiago de Chile, exploring their architectural and
symbolic dimensions through a semiotic framework. These high-density residential towers,
shaped by market-driven urban policies, exemplify the intersection of neoliberalism and socio-
spatial inequality. By analysing floor plans, sections and elevations, the study reveals how
architectural decisions prioritise economic efficiency over community well-being, reinforcing
exclusion and marginalisation. Modular repetition, centralised circulation, and the absence of
meaningful communal spaces encode socioeconomic hierarchies into the built environment,
transforming these structures into instruments of segregation and control. Situating vertical
ghettos as material and ideological expressions of neoliberal urbanism, this research advances
urban studies by integrating semiotics to decode the hidden meanings of contemporary housing
typologies. The findings highlight the urgency of inclusive urban policies and architectural
practices that challenge housing commodification and address the sociopolitical tensions shaping
modern urban landscapes.

Introduction
Neoliberal urbanism has profoundly reconfigured contemporary cities, imposing market-driven
logics that commodify housing, exacerbating socio-spatial inequalities. These transformations
are conspicuously materialised in spatial examples such as vertical ghettos, understood as high-
density residential towers which, though promoted as solutions to housing crises, effectively
deepen processes of exclusion, control and stigmatisation. Rather than mere architectural prod-
ucts, these towers constitute symbolic and material embodiments of neoliberal rationality,
wherein economic imperatives eclipse human well-being. This research critically examines these
structures as semiotic constructs, seeking to unravel how spatial configurations and architectural
choices articulate broader sociopolitical narratives.

The term ghetto historically denotes urban enclaves of marginalised groups created through
enforced segregation and discrimination. Contemporary uses encompass diverse contexts, cap-
turing neighbourhoods characterised by structural marginalisation and limited resource access
(Marcuse, 1997). These spaces emerge through complex intersections of race, economics, and
institutional governance, which urban theorists have extensively explored. For instance, Hirsch’s
second ghetto thesis emphasises systemic mechanisms perpetuating racial and economic segre-
gation, while recent critiques underscore the racial-capitalist foundations underpinning urban
inequalities (Gilfoyle, 2020; Jenkins, 2020; Montalva Barba, 2023).
However, understanding the persistence and evolution of ghettos requires examining urban
design as a critical determinant of spatial inequality. Harris et al. (2023) highlight how architec-
tural practices can either mitigate or reinforce social divisions, critiquing design choices prioritis-
ing commercial interests over community cohesion. Urban planning decisions significantly
affect the lived experiences of marginalised populations, intensifying environmental degrada-
tion, health disparities, and social alienation (Cushing et al., 2015; Sloane, 2019).
Verticalisation epitomises these contradictions inherent in neoliberal urbanism (Graham,
2015; Sudjic, 2006). Across diverse global contexts – from Brisbane’s densification policies to
Shiraz’s market-driven expansion – the economic efficiency often associated with vertical devel-
opments frequently reinforces social inequalities (Azhdari et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023).
Similarly, verticalisation’s impacts in Santiago, particularly in the district of Estación Central,
starkly illustrate how speculative real estate, aided by regulatory flexibility, prioritises financial
interests over communal well-being, resulting in inadequate infrastructure, congestion and inten-
sified socio-spatial segregation (Urbina Julio, 2024).
In Santiago, vertical ghettos manifest prominently in Estación Central, although not exclu-
sively. Initially perceived as affordable, centrally located housing, these towers swiftly became
symbolic markers of marginalisation due to overcrowding, substandard living conditions and
stigma (Brain, 2025). Regulatory loopholes allowed real estate developers to construct multiple
contiguous buildings under separate legal entities, circumventing density restrictions and creat-
ing spaces characterised by anonymity, isolation and structural precarity (Aubán Borrell &
Campos Medina, 2023; Rojas Symmes, 2022).
To systematically decode the symbolic meanings embedded in these urban forms, this study
employs semiotics, drawing particularly from Barthes’s and Eco’s conceptions of signification
(Aroni, 2023; Gottdiener & Lagopoulos, 2019). Semiotics offers tools to dissect the ideological
narratives inscribed within architectural layouts, identifying connotative (cultural) and denota-
tive (literal) meanings in spatial configurations. Through detailed analyses of floor plans, eleva-
tions and sections, the study investigates how design choices – such as modular repetition,
centralised circulation systems and minimal communal spaces – encode market logics and rein-
force social hierarchies.

Specifically, the analysis of buildings on Santa Petronila street reveals explicit semiotic
dimensions of spatial exclusion. Uniform floor plans, standardised apartment layouts and mini-
mal communal areas prioritise economic efficiency over liveability, embedding depersonalisa-
tion and isolation into the physical environment. Elevations reflect visual homogeneity,
reinforcing anonymity and stigma, while architectural sections underscore density-driven strate-
gies that compromise social interactions. Collectively, these elements constitute a semiotic nar-
rative of neoliberal urbanism, transforming housing into transactional commodities rather than
social goods.
The significance of this semiotic approach extends beyond critique; it provides critical insights
for policy and practice. Understanding the symbolic and material interplay in architectural forms
highlights how urban governance and planning practices contribute directly to socio-spatial
injustices. Semiotics thus presents itself as a powerful analytical tool capable of informing more
inclusive, culturally attuned urban policies that challenge speculative real estate models.
Ultimately, this research advances urban studies by exposing how architectural and spatial
configurations in vertical ghettos serve as material texts that narrate the contradictions of neolib-
eral urbanism. By integrating semiotic methodologies, it underscores the necessity for urban
governance frameworks grounded in equity, participatory design, and collective well-being,
resisting the commodification and exclusion inherent in contemporary urban development
paradigms.
The study of vertical ghettos underscores the urgent need for alternative urban policies that
challenge the commodification of housing and prioritise inclusivity. By applying semiotics to
urban analysis, this research unveils the deeper meanings embedded in contemporary housing
typologies, advancing critical debates on spatial inequality in global cities.
The semiotic reading of vertical ghettos does more than deconstruct architectural form; it
provides urban policy-makers with a diagnostic tool for uncovering the hidden narratives that
shape residents’ everyday experiences (Gonzalez, 2025). By decoding the signs embedded in
floor plans, façades, and circulation schemas – whether through modular repetition that signals
anonymity or the erasure of communal thresholds that constrains social interaction – semiotics
makes visible the cultural assumptions and power relations that standard technical reports often
overlook (Aroni, 2023; Barthes, 1990). When planners and city officials engage with these find-
ings, they can trace how built elements communicate exclusion or belonging and, crucially, how
residents interpret and even subvert these messages in practice. This grounded insight bridges the
epistemic divide between expert decision-makers and local communities: rather than imposing
top-down zoning prescriptions, authorities can facilitate semiotic workshops in which stakehold-
ers annotate plans, share personal readings of space and co-construct new symbols of collective
identity. Such participatory mapping exercises not only inform more inclusive regulatory frame-
works – by, for example, mandating diverse communal typologies or varied façade articulations
– but also restore a sense of agency to neighbourhood groups. In this way, semiotics becomes the
connective tissue between governance and lived place-making, guiding urbanisation processes
that honour local imaginaries, resist homogenising market pressures, and ultimately foster neigh-
bourhoods where material form and social aspiration converge.
Verticalisation as Urban Phenomenon
Drawing on critical scholarship that interrogates the logics of market-led urbanism, several stud-
ies illuminate the entanglement of verticalisation with neoliberal governance. Tafuri (1976) con-
tends that avant-garde architectural utopias were ultimately co-opted by capitalist development,
transforming high-rise housing into instruments of speculative capital rather than vehicles of
social emancipation. Minton (2013) further exposes how the enclosure of public space in con-
temporary cities – through gated high-rise developments and controlled communal areas

– manifests an ‘architecture of fear’ that intensifies social divisions and erodes civic life. Rykwert
(2001) warns that the placelessness engendered by uniform vertical blocks alienates inhabitants
from history and community, undermining the city’s capacity to foster meaningful intersubjec-
tive bonds. Newman (1972) provide a critical insight to this argument when saying that high-
density towers, when designed without attention to defensible communal thresholds, exacerbate
perceptions of insecurity and weaken collective territorial stewardship. Eco’s (2012) semiotic
analysis of architectural form reveals that tall buildings communicate an ideology of efficiency
and control which, when uncritically embraced, naturalises market imperatives as inevitable
urban truths (Arias-Loyola & Vergara-Perucich, 2021). Verticalisation is not a neutral response
to land scarcity but a strategic urban assemblage that privileges capital accumulation, employs
coercive planning instruments and reconfigures social relations in accordance with ideological
mantras, such as the neoliberal rationalities in Chile.
The neoliberal urbanisation model has significantly impacted urban development and gover-
nance. Large-scale urban development projects (UDPs) have been used to implement exceptional
planning measures, often favouring elite interests over democratic participation (Swyngedouw
et al., 2002). This approach, part of the New Urban Policy, has led to socioeconomic polarisation
through real estate market dynamics and shifts in public budget priorities (Swyngedouw et al.,
2002). Neoliberal planning tends to prioritise economic growth over social needs, limiting the
poor’s right to the city and reducing planning to market facilitation (Baffoe, 2023). However,
some scholars argue that neoliberalism’s influence on urban transformation may be overstated
and that cities change for various reasons (Galès, 2016). To address these issues, there is a call
for more inclusive planning approaches, particularly in Global South cities, to create more equi-
table urban spaces (Baffoe, 2023).
The relationship between urban verticalisation and the economy is complex, shaped by plan-
ning policies, market forces and socioeconomic contexts. In Brisbane, verticalisation is driven by
parcel amalgamation and densification policies, though their impact remains limited without
broader strategies (Huang et al., 2023). In Shiraz, Azhdari et al. (2024) observe that market forces
surpass government planning, increasing density but failing to ensure infrastructure and equita-
ble access to services, deepening inequalities. In Belém, Lima et al. (2021) show how macroeco-
nomic fluctuations and housing programmes like Minha Casa Minha Vida have made
verticalisation both a response to demand and a vehicle for real estate speculation, exacerbating
territorial disparities.
Wang et al. (2022) examine high-rise developments in the Pearl River Delta, finding they raise
land values through accessibility and urban quality, benefitting some while excluding others. In
Brazil, Belmiro et al. (2023) argue that floor-area ratio (FAR) intensification may encourage
sprawl, disproportionately impacting low-income families. Fernando (2024) highlights how
state-led urban regeneration in Sri Lanka displaced vulnerable communities into high-rise hous-
ing, worsening marginalisation during the COVID-19 pandemic. In Santiago, Urbina reveals
how corporate landlords, incentivised by densification subsidies, have turned housing into a
financial asset, deepening social exclusion (Urbina Julio, 2024).
Verticalisation reshapes the skyline and also the very fabric of everyday existence, negotiating
residents’ sense of dignity and self-perception through the configuration of space and social rela-
tions. In Hong Kong’s high-rise precincts, Peng and Maing (2021) show that the paucity of acces-
sible open areas severs vital connections to nature and community, eroding older adults’
well-being even as poorly calibrated façades amplify heat stress – an architectural disregard that
communicates the expendability of vulnerable bodies. Lau (2024) further reveals that digital cor-
ridors and generational divides in Singapore’s vertical estates produce parallel lives: youth retreat
into virtual worlds while elders are left stranded on balconies, their stories unheard. Such frag-
mented co-existence corrodes the intergenerational solidarity that once anchored neighbourhood
identity. In Brussels, Mosseray et al. (2023) document how top-down planning routines render

inhabitants invisible, their quotidian practices dismissed as inconvenient anomalies rather than as
essential threads in the urban tapestry. The cumulative effect of these conditions is a depersonali-
sation of daily life – an implicit message that individual agency and collective belonging are
subordinate to the imperatives of density and capital. By foregrounding these lived dimensions,
semiotic analysis exposes how vertical form can both diminish human dignity and retrench social

More broadly, verticalisation embodies tensions between economic efficiency and social sus-
tainability. While increasing density can support urban growth, it also reinforces inequalities and
marginalises vulnerable populations. These dynamics reflect the interplay between market logic,
public policy, and architectural design, shaping cities where the benefits of verticalisation are
unevenly distributed.
Addressing verticalisation requires a spatially sensitive approach, as abstract regulations often
fail to account for local complexities. In Singapore, Cao et al. (2022) show how planning models
have produced fragmented spaces ill-suited to residents’ needs, calling for more cohesive urban
design. Adey (2024) explores how verticality amplifies risks in emergencies, where gender, class,
and race dynamics affect building design and evacuation capacity. In South Korea, Lee et al.
(2022) find that while high-rise apartments improve residential satisfaction, they hinder social
capital formation, limiting community cohesion.
Ultimately, verticalisation reshapes urban life, intertwining economic, political, and social
dimensions. While it offers efficiency and design benefits, its associated risks and inequalities
highlight the urgent need for inclusive and equitable urban planning.
The Vertical Ghettoes in Santiago de Chile
Santiago de Chile exemplifies the intersection of public policy, spatial dynamics, and daily life
in a neoliberal society (Bruey, 2020; Solimano, 2012). Housing policies aiming to eliminate
informality paradoxically sustain it through cycles of repopulation (Abufhele Milad, 2023;
Alegria & Dovey, 2022; Fuster-Farfan, 2020), reinforcing spatial divisions and targeted interven-
tions. Informal settlements demonstrate resilience, adapting beyond state and market constraints
(Inostroza, 2016; Vergara-Perucich, 2022).
Neoliberalism’s impact extends to the middle class, whose symbolic narratives reinforce
socio-spatial inequalities (Fuentes & Mac-Clure, 2020). Urban expansion generates interstitial
spaces that challenge dominant models of development and sustainability (Phelps & Silva, 2018;
Silva & Vergara-Perucich, 2021). Meanwhile, Santiago’s push for technological modernisation
prioritises elites, excluding vulnerable communities and revealing contradictions between inno-
vation and inequality (Irazábal & Jirón, 2021).
The city’s cultural composition is further reshaped by Latin American migrants, reinforcing
dynamics of exclusion (Aguirre-Nuñez et al., 2024; Inzulza Contardo et al., 2019). Sheehan
highlights how vertical enclaves serve as spaces of both segregation and adaptation (Sheehan,
2024). Santiago’s neoliberal transformation is epitomised in the vertical ghettos of Estación
Central, consolidating its role as a critical case in Latin American urban studies (Boano &
Perucich, 2017; Dattwyler et al., 2019; Garreton, 2017; Vergara-Perucich, 2019, 2021).
In Santiago de Chile, vertical ghettos epitomise the neoliberal transformation of the urban
landscape (Link et al., 2023; López-Morales et al., 2019; Rojas Symmes et al., 2023). Concentrated
in districts such as Estación Central, these high-density towers were initially conceived as com-
mercial projects providing affordable, well-located housing for middle-income residents.
However, they have instead generated new forms of housing precariousness (Aubán Borrell &
Campos Medina, 2023; Rojas-Symmes, 2017). The term ‘vertical ghetto’, popularised in 2017,
refers not only to overcrowded and deficient physical conditions but also to the symbolic stigma
linking these buildings to urban marginalisation (Aubán Borrell & Campos Medina, 2023).

Verticalisation in Santiago reflects the financialisation of urban land, where regulatory flex-
ibility has enabled real estate speculation at the expense of collective well-being (Cattaneo
Pineda, 2011; Rojas Symmes et al., 2023; Vergara, 2017; Vergara-Perucich, 2021). This has
resulted in overstretched public services, a lack of communal spaces, and increased socio-
spatial segregation. The design of these towers neglects their immediate urban environment,
leading to congested streets, inadequate recreational areas, and a disconnect from residents’
needs (Schlack & Vicuña, 2011; Vicuña & Cortillas, 2021; Vicuña del Río, 2020). These issues
contribute to insecurity, social isolation, and a lack of community cohesion, intensifying pre-
carious living conditions (Aubán Borrell & Campos Medina, 2023; Vergara-Perucich & Arias-
Loyola, 2021).
While verticalisation could address housing shortages, successful models require inclusive
and sustainable policies. Roast (2024) highlights how densification in Chongqing, China, has
integrated local communities rather than marginalising them. However, in Santiago, such inte-
gration remains aspirational rather than achievable. The gap between official narratives promot-
ing densification as progress and its real impact on communities underscores the contradictions
of neoliberal urbanism.
Vertical ghettos (Figure 1) illustrate broader urban challenges in Latin America, where neolib-
eralism shapes not just urban landscapes but also daily experiences. In Santiago, they represent a
new form of informal settlement born from real estate speculation and regulatory gaps, embed-
ding exclusionary dynamics into the city’s neoliberal fabric (Cociña & Burgos, 2017; Vergara-
Perucich, 2021). A semiotic analysis of these spaces critically reveals how their spatial
configurations materialise social exclusion, further defining Santiago’s urban identity.
Calle Santa Petronila (Figure 2), in Santiago’s Estación Central, exemplifies the unregulated
vertical densification reshaping the city. Once a low-rise residential neighbourhood, it is now
dominated by high-density towers exceeding 25 stories, where thousands of small apartments –
some as tiny as 20 square metres – accommodate an overwhelming population (Figure 3).
Originally marketed as affordable housing, these buildings instead reflect speculative real estate
practices, prioritising profit over livability.
With densities surpassing 14,000 residents per hectare, the area faces severe infrastructural
strain. Narrow sidewalks are congested with pedestrians, vendors, and parked vehicles, while pub-
lic transport and services remain insufficient. The absence of green areas and communal spaces has
turned the street into an informal public realm, marked by conflicts over space. Figure 3 illustrates
the severe spatial constraints of a 32 m2 flat in the analysed high-rise, where essential domestic
activities – walking, stretching, relaxing or interacting – are spatially restricted. The compact inte-
gration of living, dining, and kitchen areas forces residents to adapt their movements and routines
to a rigid, efficiency-driven layout, prioritising density over livability. Analysing its semiotics can
reveal how design choices communicate economic priorities over human comfort, shaping not only
physical space but also social relations, privacy, and well-being.
A Method for Semiotic Analysis on Urban Design
Building on Barthes’s revival of Saussure’s dyadic sign model, this study treats the vertical ghetto
of Estación Central as a first-order signifier whose denotative form – the geometric arrangement
of floor plans, sections and façades – must be rigorously described, before moving to a second-
order signified: the sociocultural logics they conceal (Barthes, 1990). In this respect, the study
follow Barthes’s injunction to unmask myth by making visible how standardised units, central-
ised cores and façade repetition naturalise depersonalisation and socioeconomic control as if they
were inevitable facts of urban life (Aroni, 2023; Gottdiener & Lagopoulos, 2019).
Concurrently, Eco’s dynamic semiotics highlights the reader-response dimension: just as
myths circulate and mutate in the popular imagination, so too do architectural forms accrue

In summary, this research proceeds from a rigorous case-study design to a layered semiotic
inquiry that uncovers both the material and ideological dimensions of urban form. Drawing on
Yin’s (2018) principle of exemplariness and Krehl and Weck’s (2020) notion of multiple embedded
units, the article presents the selected case of the Santa Petronila complex in Estación Central as the
focal case. Its sheer scale, the proliferation of individual towers and the unusually comprehensive
collection of architectural drawings render it simultaneously representative of contemporary verti-
cal ghettos and richly documented for in-depth analysis. Two contrasting comparator cases – a set
of high-end luxury sky-towers and a cluster of mid-rise social housing projects – anchor the inter-
pretation within Santiago’s broader residential spectrum, revealing the specific semiotic load car-
ried by Santa Petronila’s configurations.
All available floor plans, sections and elevations were re-draw from PDF files for having high
resolution and subjected to a meticulous cleaning process. Annotations and extraneous graphic
artefacts were removed, and each drawing was normalised for scale and orientation. The cleaned
vector files were then catalogued in a relational database using identifiers for block, level and
façade segment. This systematic preprocessing established a consistent graphic substrate for sub-
sequent spatial coding. Parallel to this, a fieldwork recorded everyday interactions with commu-
nal spaces. Through informal interviews, photographic documentation of corridors and stairwells
in use, and annotated notes on improvised signage and furniture arrangements, the research col-
lected qualitative data that situate architectural forms within lived practices.
The analytical workflow unfolded in two intertwined stages. First, it was applied the denota-
tive matrix to quantify unit dimensions, core placement, communal-area geometry and repetitive
façade rhythms. These parameters were compared across cases to identify patterns of spatial
hierarchy and access control. Second, drawing on Barthes’s (1990) second-order signification
and Aroni’s (2023) semiotic toolkit, the research applied an interpretation on how denoted ele-
ments function as ideological myths. Small, centralised communal nodes – nominally shared –
were revealed as contested arenas of exclusion; uniform façades emerged as myths of anonymity
naturalised through repetition. By embedding these readings within frameworks of neoliberal
urbanism, financialization and territorial stigma, the study produces a continuous protocol for

This study deliberately privileges the analysis of spatial artefacts – the precise geometry of
floor plans, sections and façades – over resident interviews or focus groups to foreground the
semiotic intensity of architectural form itself. By isolating the material signifiers embedded in
the drawings, the research aimed to trace how design decisions encode ideologies of segregation,
commodification and control without the interpretive overlay introduced by individual percep-
tions. This purely spatial approach aligns with Barthes’s call to unmask myth at the level of the
signifier (Barthes, 1990) and with Yin’s (2018) emphasis on the intrinsic value of documentary
evidence in case-study research. While interviews would undoubtedly enrich the understanding
of lived experience, their subjectivity risks conflating personal narratives with broader structural
patterns. Accordingly, a second research phase is envisioned in which ethnographic methods –
semi-structured interviews and participatory mapping – will be deployed to complement the
current findings, validating and expanding our semiotic readings with the residents’ own inter-
pretations and practices (Abasolo Llaría, 2021). This staged design ensures that the material and
symbolic dimensions of vertical ghetto design are robustly established before exploring their
reception in everyday life.

Chile’s Environmental Impact Assessment System (SEIA) regulates projects with significant
environmental impacts, including urban developments. Administered by the Environmental
Assessment Service (SEA), SEIA ensures compliance with legal and technical standards. The
Vertical Ghettos in Estación Central required SEIA registration due to their scale, particularly the
inclusion of more than 130 parking spaces per building, indicating substantial environmental and
social consequences. As a result, much of the project’s documentation, including detailed archi-
tectural plans, became publicly accessible – an exceptionally rare occurrence in Chile’s real
estate sector.
This study focuses on the most emblematic case: a residential complex located between María
Rozas, Las Rejas, and Santa Petronila streets. Spanning 13,800 m2, it contains nearly 5,000 units,
reaching an extreme density of 3,623 dwellings or approximately 14,292 residents per hectare.
These figures illustrate the radical intensity of vertical development in Santiago and the profound
urban pressures it generates.
The unlocked access to these architectural plans permits a finely grained semiotic excavation
of how the vertical ghetto materialises key tenets of neoliberal metropolitan life. Far from neutral
housing stock, its repetitive modules and compressed communal cores enact a market logic that
privileges land commodification and speculative capital over social utility (Harvey, 2005). State
and municipal authorities, collaborating with private developers, deploy coercive planning
instruments – zoning variances, density bonuses, infrastructure subsidies – to shepherd invest-
ment into these towers, effectively externalising social costs onto vulnerable populations (Brenner
& Theodore, 2002). In this framing, the vertical ghetto stands as a paradigmatic ‘assemblage’ of
profit-driven urban design: individual units are marketed as isolated assets in a financial portfo-
lio, communal spaces are minimised to maximise sellable floor area, and standardised façades
project a branding of ‘efficient modernity’ that masks deep inequalities. The technical drawings
thus encode a dual message: denotatively, they map FARs and core-to-unit metrics; connota-
tively, they narrate a neoliberal myth in which urban life is reduced to transactional interactions
among atomized tenants and capital flows. By decoding these layers, the study reveals how state-
sanctioned densification policies not only reproduce spatial segregation but also legitimise an
exclusionary vision of city-making centred on private profit, regulatory capture and the erosion
of collective urban rights.

The floor plan of the vertical ghetto (Figure 4), analysed semiotically, prioritises space efficiency
and technical functionality. Residential units follow a rigid, standardised layout to maximise

approach prioritises economic efficiency over social cohesion. On a connotative level, modular
repetition depersonalises residents, reducing housing to a transactional commodity. The lack of
shared spaces reinforces social isolation, embedding segregation into the built environment. As a
semiotic text (Bianchi, 2011), the floor plan reflects the tensions between real estate priorities
and housing needs, perpetuating exclusion and marginalisation in Santiago’s neoliberal urban
landscape.
The elevation in Figure 5, at a denotative level, presents a modular and repetitive design char-

sense of homogeneity across the building. The vertical scale and absence of significant material
or architectural variations reflect a functional and economic approach, devoid of distinctive or
identity-oriented elements. Connotatively, this elevation conveys the standardisation linked to
mass housing production in dense urban contexts, where repetition symbolises a prioritisation of
economic efficiency over diversity and uniqueness. The design lacks elements that humanise the
façade, reinforcing anonymity and depersonalisation – common traits in vertical ghettos. The
monumental scale and lack of differentiation between units underscore dynamics of social exclu-

ity and absence of visible spaces for communal interaction suggest physical and symbolic
isolation, reinforcing the spatial and social hierarchies inherent in the neoliberal urbanisation
model.

The Meaning of the Vertical Ghetto
The semiotic analysis of floor plans, elevations and sections from Santiago’s vertical ghettos
confirms that design logics of modular repetition and compressed communal spaces resonate
with broader critiques of financialised urban development. Vergara-Perucich (2021) argues that
housing commodification under neoliberal regimes transforms dwellings into fungible assets, a
process vividly illustrated by the interchangeable unit layouts observed in Figures 4 to 6. These
standardised plans, prioritising sellable area over resident well-being, echo Urbina Julio’s (2024)
findings that densification subsidies incentivise developers to maximise FARs at the expense of
social infrastructure. Such configurations align with Cattaneo Pineda’s (2011) concept of specu-
lative territorialisation, wherein regulatory flexibility is exploited to accelerate profit-driven con-
struction, leaving minimal space for collective amenities.
The conspicuous absence of varied communal nodes within the analysed sections highlights
how these towers may inhibit social interaction, supporting Harris et al.’s (2023) critique that
neoliberal design often erodes community cohesion. Whereas Newman’s (1972) defensible space
theory would advocate clear territorial thresholds to foster stewardship, the homogenised cores
documented here offer neither semi-private thresholds nor welcoming gathering spaces. This

of a transactional architecture, reflecting Lefebvre’s (1991; Coleman, 2015) thesis that rational-

Elevations marked by monotonous façades reinforce a visual mythology of efficiency, a phe-
nomenon Schmid (2022) describes as architectural encoding of power structures. The symmetri-
cal grid of balconies and windows conveys an ideology of uniformity, mirroring Wacquant’s

legible place-markers or distinctive identities, these façades erase difference, underscoring

this light, the vertical ghetto emerges as a semiotic text that naturalises speculative logics.
Vertical ghettos in Estación Central are not neutral responses to land scarcity but deliberate
spatial embodiments of neoliberal rationality. Through denotative patterns – repetitive modules,
centralised cores and minimal shared areas – and their connotative myths of efficiency and ano-
nymity, these towers crystallise the tensions between capital imperatives and communal well-
being documented across the literature. Semiotic analysis thus offers a possible definition of the
vertical ghetto typology.
The semiotic reading of Santiago’s vertical ghettos identifies a series of formal choices –
modular repetition, compressed FARs and the near absence of shared amenities – that align
closely with market-driven imperatives in Chilean urbanism. By focusing on plans, sections and
elevations, this study demonstrates that repeated unit typologies and minimised communal cores
can be understood as strategies to maximise sellable area rather than to cultivate social interac-
tion. Such configurations do not, by themselves, prove processes of dispossession or measured

increases in inequality, but they do signal a design logic in which economic efficiency is priori-
tised over collective well-being.
Although the towers’ standardised façades and circulation schemas cannot alone confirm
exclusionary intent, they do suggest an architectural vocabulary of anonymity and control. The
lack of diverse communal thresholds tends to limit spontaneous encounters, pointing to a spatial
form that may discourage the emergence of neighbourhood solidarities. These features resonate
with broader critiques of financialised urban development – where densification often proceeds
without integrated social infrastructure (Urbina Julio, 2024; Vergara-Perucich, 2021; Vergara-
Perucich & Boano, 2024)—yet our findings remain tethered to the semiotic evidence at hand
rather than to untested claims about lived inequality.
Where the research can most confidently speak is to the coherence of these patterns as an ideo-
logical ‘script’ embedded within the drawings themselves. The repetition of identical plan mod-
ules and the erasure of distinguishing details convey a message of interchangeability that mirrors
speculative logics in real-estate markets. This does not definitively show that residents experi-
ence dispossession, but it does foreground the symbolic dimensions of such developments: hous-
ing rendered as a fungible commodity.
A subsequent research phase – incorporating interviews, focus groups and policy discourse
analysis – will be necessary to trace how these formal signs translate into everyday experiences
and perceptions. Such mixed-methods work will allow us to test whether the semiotic patterns
identified here also manifest as concrete effects on social cohesion, resident agency and claims
to the city.
Conclusion
The denotative and connotative readings conducted in this study demonstrate that the architec-
tural form of Santiago’s vertical ghettos – manifested through repetitive floor plan modules,
uniform façades and the near absence of shared amenities – articulates a spatial logic aligned
with market-driven imperatives. Rather than simply optimising land use, these configurations
signal a preference for economic efficiency that can depersonalise residents and curtail opportu-
nities for social interaction, echoing broader patterns of speculative real estate production in
Chile (Vergara-Perucich, 2021).
By confining the analysis to planimetric and elevation data, this research foregrounds the
semiotic potency of built form but inevitably omits the experiential dimension of everyday life in
these towers. Without direct engagement with inhabitants or policy-makers, the study cannot
conclusively determine how the identified signifiers translate into lived perceptions of exclusion
or agency. Nor does it capture the normative forces and political negotiations that enabled these
developments.
Future work should therefore integrate qualitative methods – interviews, focus groups and
participatory mapping – to validate and enrich the semiotic interpretations presented here. Such
a mixed-methods approach will clarify the extent to which architectural signs of anonymity and
control correspond with residents’ narratives and urban governance practices. Ultimately, by
combining rigorous semiotic analysis with grounded sociological inquiry, scholars and practitio-
ners can better diagnose – and challenge – the exclusionary spatial dynamics that vertical ghettos
inscribe into the contemporary city.

Design in Development from The Bartlett Development Planning Unit, and a PhD from University College
London. He currently directs the “Centro Producción del Espacio” (Centre for the Production of Space)
Research Nucleus at the Universidad de Las Américas, Chile, and is a professor in the Master’s in Socio-
spatial Practices programme at the same institution. He is the principal investigator for the UDLA internal
