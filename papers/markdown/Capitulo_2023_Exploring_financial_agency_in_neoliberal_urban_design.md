Introduction
Ethos refers to the guiding beliefs and ideals that characterise the behaviour of a certain group
of individuals. In this case, the ethos of the urban designer refers to the set of beliefs and atti-
tudes of urban design practitioners when deciding on urban form and space shaping. As a
practice-oriented discipline, the decisions made by urban designers have physical representa-
tions in space which are long-lasting in urban life. For Alexander Cuthbert, urban designers
must deal with the political sphere and understand that every single change in space is a
political action (A. R. Cuthbert, 2006, 2011). As Sorkin argues, urban design has assumed an
orthodox which has epistemological implications as urban theory has not advanced towards
more imaginative ways of seeing urban life (Sorkin, 2013). This condition of this discipline
field may be a consequence of the assumption of urban design as an economic driver only
(F. Vergara-Perucich, 2019), which implied different methodological changes (Carmona &
Tiesdell, 2007; Larice & Macdonald, 2011). Urban design, as a disciplinary field, cannot be
subsumed under a total methodology that eliminates the richness of conflicts for defining the
methods to study the urban form (A. Cuthbert, 2007). Generalisations on urban design are
discussable because methodologies are not fixed but also because each city has diversity and
complexities that are unique. However, many times the analysis of urban design processes
neglects the economic features of its practice, motivations, and social effects. Therefore,
what happens when the ethos of urban designers embraces the aims of neoliberalism? How
the complexity of neoliberal urban processes can be tackled through research strategies? This
chapter offers an approach to this question.
Neoliberalism as a concept refers to laissez-faire economics, whose goals are economic
liberalisation, defence of private property rights, governmental austerity, and free trade
(Azevedo et al., 2019; Bourdieu, 1998). Neoliberalism situates the private (individual, enter-
prises) at the centre of economic activities.
At a base level we can say that when we refer to ‘neoliberalism’, we are generally refer-
ring to the new political, economic and social arrangements within society that empha-
size market relations, re-tasking the role of the state, and individual responsibility. Most

scholars tend to agree that neoliberalism is broadly defined as the extension of competi-
tive markets into all areas of life, including the economy, politics and society.
(Springer, 2016, p. 2)
In practice, neoliberalism is also a governmental model that eliminates control over prices,
deregulates capital markets, fosters free international trade, and advances toward the privati-
sation of social security (Boas & Gans-Morse, 2009). David Harvey’s definition of neoliber-
alism says that neoliberalism is ‘either a utopian project to realises a theoretical design for the
reorganisation of international capitalism or a political project to re-establish the conditions
for capital accumulation and to restore the power of economic elites’ (Harvey, 2005, p. 19).
Neoliberalism fosters individualism, and one of the main spatial expressions of individualised
space is the dwelling with its spatial representation: housing. Under neoliberalism, the push for
homeownership by governments has been a common feature (Rossi & Vanolo, 2015). Starting
in the early 1990s, after the Washington consensus, banks reduced the requirements for credits
on mortgages, which facilitated access to homeownership at a time that involved the financial
sector more actively in the production of spaces (Fernandez & Aalbers, 2016). The access of
low-income communities to multiple mortgages without financial background and the coer-
cion of the financial sector to maximise the earnings from the housing market were the main
causes of the great financial crisis of 2008 (Follain & Giertz, 2012). Indeed, the greatest crisis
facing neoliberalism emerged from the processes of space production and the participation
of financial institutions in the process (Allon & Redden, 2012; Christophers, 2019; Rolnik,
2013). Therefore, a critical theorisation of neoliberalism from the perspective of urban design
seems like a suitable approach to unpacking these ideological project-affected cities. Because
of the subprime crisis of 2008 and the real estate bubbles in Europe, neoliberalism entered a
profound crisis of credibility, and after the pandemic, it will face a new epistemological crisis
because of the incapacity of free-market economics to provide universal welfare in times of
planetary threat. Also, climate emergency causes point to the way of production of neoliberal
society as the main driver of global warming, so the survival of neoliberalism to its contra-
dictions is at stake. As Slavoj Zizek said, it seems easier to imagine the end of life on Earth
than a modest transformation in the neoliberal modes of production (Zizek, 2012). However,
nowadays it seems that global leaders are open to changing this way of thinking.
Consequently, the manifold forms and pathways of neoliberal urbanization should be
seen not as coherent, sustainable solutions to the embedded regulatory dilemmas and
contradictions of contemporary capitalism, but rather as deeply contradictory restruc-
turing strategies that are significantly destabilizing inherited landscapes of urban gover-
nance and socioeconomic regulation.
(Peck et al., 2009, p. 64)
Neoliberalisation of urban design accelerated the process through which the city became a
simple commodity (Stanek, 2008), so enquiring into its spatial forms may represent a method
for contesting the adverse effects of this ideology in urban life. John Rennie Short explains
that the neoliberal agenda has undercut the funding for urban design in the sense that the
public sector could advance toward providing a good life through urban spaces (Short, 2006).
For him, urban disciplines have been refashioned by neoliberalism in order to generate eco-
nomic growth through urban development.
An urban design model under a neoliberal regime was implemented for the first
time in Chile, particularly in Santiago, by a new urban policy introduced in 1979

Methods
This chapter takes as a case study the real estate development in Santiago de Chile to analyse
how this process delivers highly profitable outcomes while also producing gentrification and
segregation. This chapter also enlights how the urban form suffers from the financialised
model of housing provision, in which the dwellings are used as an investment rather than for
living in (J. F. Vergara-Perucich & Aguirre-Nuñez, 2019b). The data used comes from the
environmental declarations of real estate companies, the Private Property Register of Santi-
ago, and the National Institute of Statistics.
Santiago de Chile is a good case for illustrating the neoliberal contradiction as it was the
first global capital where a neoliberal urban policy was implemented (Bohoslavsky et al., 2021;
Brenner et al., 2012; Harvey, 2005). This urban policy relied on free-market economics to
shape the city that produces a highly segregated urban space (Borsdorf et al., 2016; Figueroa et
al., 2021; Sabatini et al., 2017; Toro & Orozco, 2018), triggering gentrification (Contreras &
Venegas, 2016; R. C. Hidalgo, 2020; Lopez-Morales, 2011) and reproducing urban inequality
(Martinez & Santibanez, 2015; Rodriguez et al., 2014; Ruiz-Tagle, 2016). While the profit-
ability of urban investments in the city is very high (J. F. Vergara-Perucich & Aguirre-Nuñez,
2019a; J. F. Vergara-Perucich & Boano, 2019), the benefits of free-market urban economics
have not been equally distributed and the practitioners are due to advance in understanding
their role in this process to have the power to revert these socio-spatial contradictions.
The method has a first statistical section that aims to know the potential profitability
expectations set by real estate agents to decide to invest in projects found in the city. For this
purpose, a model was developed that consists of three parts: A construction cost model, an
income model and finally a general cash flow model of the projects under study. In Chile,
this is the mechanism most used by real estate companies when deciding whether to invest
in a housing project or not. Subsequently, the second section provides evidence of how these
profit-driven urban designs generate social impacts in this city, using literature on the case
of Santiago de Chile.
The method assesses the total financial efficiency of the investment. This profit is analysed
through the internal rate of return (IRR) and the net present value (NPV). The construction
cost model is based on a budget estimation equivalent to the replacement cost validation model
of the Chilean Internal Revenue Service (SII for its Spanish initials). It is considered that the
construction of the building takes one floor per month in advance. Time value is fundamental
for correctly assessing the profitability calculations of each analysed real estate project.
The applied method is based on Vergara-Perucich and Aguirre (2019). The NPV is a
procedure that calculates the present value of a certain number of future financial flows
generated by an investment. It is calculated by applying a current discount rate to all future
cash flows, simulating what the gains would be if the cash flows were brought to the present
to compare these gains with the initial investment. The discount rate (d) is the result of the
product between the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) and the inflation rate for
the period. For these projects, a discount rate of 12% has been applied based on the reference

Case study: housing profit in Santiago de Chile
Eight real estate projects located in different districts of Santiago de Chile were analysed.
These eight projects were chosen because they had a similar architectural typology, were
built in 2017, and because they had all the financial data for each one, from building per-
mits to flyers advertising the sale of the flats. This level of detail in the information made it
possible to reconstruct the economic flow of each case. These projects are high-rise housing
buildings whose urban impact requires an environmental impact statement, so part of their
data is available for public consultation. As a summary of the cases studied, the average land
value is found to be 325 USD/sqm, for a height composed of an average of 25 floors. On aver-
age, each of the eighth buildings analysed offered 590 flats for sale with a land value of each
flat of 1598 USD/sqm. On average, the companies invested USD 21 million in each project
expecting an average profit of 36% of this initial investment. Each project has specifications
that are summarised in Table 1.10.1.

the cases studied, the production of unaffordable housing for the neighbours of the pro-

As Cuthbert argues, urban design cannot ignore the economic factors behind its practice
(A. Cuthbert, 2010; A. R. Cuthbert, 2006, 2011). Financial literacy is tremendously use-
ful for urban designers not to become complicit in processes of socio-spatial deterioration.
In the case of Santiago, the city has been nominated by the OECD as the most segregated
among the member countries (OECD, 2013). Also, different processes of precarisation of the
urban fabric as a result of free-market urban planning have been identified in the literature
(R. Hidalgo et al., 2008). Gentrification and displacement are also indicated as the results
of years using this neoliberal urban design approach (Contreras & Venegas, 2016; Lopez-
Morales, 2011,2016). Most of the literature here referred to the case of Santiago and points
out the social effects of the neoliberal urban development process. Recent evidence indicates
the statistical significance of financial institutions over housing prices and urban form in this
metropolis (J. F. Vergara-Perucich, 2021a; J. F. Vergara-Perucich, 2021b). Thus, it would
be desirable to include a financial agency in the curricular training of urban designers and
architects, to identify predatory pricing, potential displacement, and gentrification processes
before construction work begins.
For Roberts and Ng (2012), an agency relationship is a contract under which one or more
persons engage another person to perform some service on their behalf, which involves del-
egating some decision-making authority to the agent. In this case, the agency comes from
the financiers to the urban designers in developing a profitable product for their rent claims.
By positing the importance of a financial agency for urban designers, the aim is to provide

knowledge embedded in the design of urban products that not only looks at the goodness
of numbers for those investors but can also assess the social impacts of such products. In this
way, a financial agency can be produced when urban designers are able to explain to their
financiers that there are other ways of renting without falling into the search for maximum
capital gain that harms the common good.
Urban design under neoliberalism was made possible, in part, by the financial illiteracy of
urban designers, who uncritically adopted rent-seeking from projects as their own objectives,
without understanding the impacts on the common good of this mode of operation (Boano
& Perucich, 2017; Brites, 2017; Christodoulou & Lada, 2017; Encinas et al., 2019; R. Hidalgo
et al., 2014; Letelier-Troncoso et al., 2019; Theodore et al., 2009; F. Vergara-Perucich, 2019;
F. Vergara-Perucich & Boano, 2020b). To strip neoliberalism from the disciplinary practice
of urban design, it is key to move towards a financial literacy of practitioners that allows them
to the agency for the common when facing investors and authorities.
