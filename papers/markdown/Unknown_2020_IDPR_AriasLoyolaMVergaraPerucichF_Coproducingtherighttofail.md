‘Ever tried. Ever failed. No matter. Try again. Fail again. Fail better’
Samuel Beckett, ‘Worstward Ho’
‘Elegir nos hace libres, elegir nos hace fuertes’
(Choosing makes us free, choosing makes us strong)
Skimi, ‘Como átomos’
The article depicts how the inhabitants of the macrocampamento (macro-slum) ‘Los Arenales’ aimed
to bring their concrete urban utopia closer, by co-producing the first cooperative bakery in a Chilean
informal settlement. Despite its many flaws, the process is proposed as a social innovation, since it
managed to bring a new diverse economy into existence while also improving several aspects of the
urban displaced. It is also novel in gathering historically disjointed actors, such as the campamento’s
inhabitants, local NGOs, academia and the state, especially rare considering the Chilean neoliberal
context. The experimental nature of the process and inexperience of the actors involved led to several
failures. But it also co-produced unexpected outcomes, such as increased resilience, empowerment and
broader/stronger networks for the campamentos’ inhabitants, highlighting the importance of the right to
fail. The experience is presented through an engaged research approach, raising some critical assess-
ments of each actor’s participation and valuable lessons for similar endeavours.
Keywords: cooperativism, co-production, informal settlements, social innovation, grassroot resilience,
Chile, campamento, diverse economies, slums, grassroot empowerment

Introduction
The opening quote from Nobel Prize winner Samuel Beckett has been frequently
used by influential entrepreneurs and self-help gurus to highlight the idea that success
lies in better failures. However, Beckett was not known for his optimism but for being
a rather bleak and darkly humorous author. A pessimistic reading is that after each
failure, only further failure awaits, while other – less gloomy – interpretations draw
attention to a message of existential resistance, of the need to keep moving forward
no matter what. The experience analysed here somehow incorporates all of these,
specifically regarding the multi-actor process of co-producing the first cooperative
bakery in an informal Chilean settlement, known as a campamento (Ojeda et al., 2018).
The second quote is not as famous (yet) and comes from Skimi, a young rapper
and artist from the macrocampamento Los Arenales;1 the largest conglomerate of campa-
mentos in the city of Antofagasta, Chile, known worldwide for its outstanding mineral
wealth and macroeconomic growth (Arias et al., 2014; OECD, 2013; Phelps et al.,
2015). As a promised land of mining-related jobs, Antofagasta has attracted Latin
American immigrants who aspire to get better wages and living conditions (Rodrigo-
Benito, 2010). However, the city also faces a steady increase in the cost of living,
especially related to housing (Vergara-Perucich and Boano, 2019); relatively low wages;
and high unemployment rates (Durán and Kremerman, 2019). These factors have
forced immigrant workers to settle in informal settlements (MINVU, 2019), exposed
to a multi-layered stigmatisation characterised by social exclusion, xenophobia and
aporophobia (Cortina, 2017; Tijoux, 2016; Tijoux and Díaz, 2014).
As a migrant herself, Skimi’s art represents the struggle of the people of Los Arenales
for their right to the city. Under the motto ‘por la casa, por un techo, la vivienda es un
derecho’,2 these dwellers struggle to overcome their extreme precarity through collective
organisation and self-sufficiency. They do not want to be passive receivers of top-down
solutions, but to build their urban space by developing new means of production.
Skimi’s lyrics encapsulate their aspirations when she sings that choosing makes them
free, stronger and eager to learn, experiment and overcome any setbacks.
In 2017, some community members of Los Arenales chose to start the first cooper-
ative bakery in Chile, the birthplace of neoliberalism (Harvey, 2005; Klein, 2007).3
This was an experimental multi-actor effort which may be understood as a social
innovation process for co-producing a novel form of diverse economy (Bell and Pahl,
2018; Gibson-Graham, 2008), where several disjointed local, national and interna-
tional actors came together in an extremely unlikely alliance, involving macrocampa-

This article’s objective is to describe and assess the turbulent but inspiring process of
failing, trying again, and failing a bit better in co-producing the bakery named Coopera-
tiva Intercultural de Trabajo CINTRA Los Arenales.4 Against all odds, the process
managed to co-produce a symbolic and elemental product, bread (Vergara-Perucich
and Arias-Loyola, 2019) and to deliver valuable lessons regarding how to implement
experimental and cooperative modes of production from informality, based on solidarity,
self-sufficiency, resilience and urban social justice (Bell and Pahl, 2018; Mitlin, 2008).
As depicted in Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola (2019), the process was perme-
ated by the ‘right to the city agenda’, communal thinking of the concrete utopia approach,
meaning the multi-actor efforts and plans to advance from solidary reflections to their
actual realisation (Lefebvre, 1968). The bakery faced internal and external frictions but,
despite those challenges, it ignited a process where Los Arenales’ inhabitants were empow-
ered by solidifying its inner interactions, expanded their networks, fostered a more favour-
able public opinion towards the campamentos, learned from the mistakes and facilitated new
chances for co-producing valuable inputs for bargaining with the Chilean state.
The experimental nature of the project, despite its flaws, led to several unexpected
outcomes, such as improved solidarity, empowerment and resilience. This accentuates
the critical importance of the right to fail, to keep trying, and fail better. By overcoming
difficulties, the group also co-created mutual trust and a solid feeling of commitment
to the success of the bakery. This is one of the most innovative aspects of the experi-
ence and findings.
An exemplary consequence of those newly developed, multi-actor relations and
organisational capabilities was that the funding by the Slum Dwellers International
NGO in Chile for the first Know Your City project was awarded to Los Arenales,
Rompiendo Barreras in 2018.5 This led to co-producing primary data used in their
bargaining with the state to achieve their desired radicación gaining local and national
notoriety.6 These experiences strengthened the possibility of their being recognised as
part of the city while becoming a reference for innovation, organised solidarity and
resilience for other campamentos.
The article is structured as follows: First, it discusses the co-production of a diverse
economy based on cooperation, solidarity and self-sufficiency. Second, the methodolo-
gies of engaged research and empirical data are detailed; Third, it presents the context
of the bakery. Fourth, it describes how the bakery project began. Fifth, it analyses the
process and provides learning for future endeavours focused on fostering urban social
justice. Finally, the article summarises the main conclusions and reflections.

The social innovation of co-producing a diverse economy in
an informal settlement
There is well-established literature about experimental social innovations led by
impoverished grassroots communities to find novel and sustainable forms of social
justice. This article assesses the struggle experienced by a group of slum dwellers
in their creation of a self-sufficient, concrete utopia of their choosing, based on a
similar strategy, but more politically engaged with the aims of the community rather
than individuals (Lefebvre, 1968; Purcell, 2002; Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola,
2019). This meant diversifying the local economy from a purely capitalistic standpoint
focused on large-scale mining exploitation by introducing the first cooperative bakery
in a Chilean macrocampamento (Gibson-Graham, 2008; Gibson-Graham et al., 2013).
To do this, the community reached out to a local NGO, the state and academics,
all of which participated in co-creating the bakery, thus learning substantially from
the process (Bell and Pahl, 2018; Mitlin, 2008; 2018; Ojeda et al., 2018). This section
briefly summarises the theoretical framework to reflect on how this bakery contributes
to knowledge.
Social innovations for better futures
For some, the concept of social innovation is considered a ‘buzz word’ or ‘plastic
concept’, void of any real meaning (Van der Laan, 2001). Nonetheless, it could be
useful for describing the implementation of experimental new ideas with the capacity
to spark cultural, normative or regulative changes, reinforce a society’s collective
power and improve its economic outcomes, social performance and resilience. The
concept also implies efforts to bring society closer to a utopic future (Heiskala, 2007;
Pol and Ville, 2009). To concretise that utopia, social innovations focus on improving
the quality and/or the quantity of life, putting human welfare in the spotlight, whereas
the other kind, business innovations, only focus on increasing the firm’s profits (Pol and
Ville, 2009).
Consequently, a desirable social innovation is an original and socially implemented
idea that presents convincing evidence about improving the macro-quality of life.
They should increase the number of beneficial options available to them in terms
of living, working and political and environmental (among others) conditions made
available through market/non-market mechanisms. In this sense, social innovations
fit better with collective forms of production (Pol and Ville, 2009).
Social innovations could be crucial in assessing some burning socio-economic
issues, especially related to extreme concentration of wealth and outdated visions of
eternal economic growth. Those issues might threaten socio-economic ecosystems
and foster the displacement of people to informal urban spaces, feeding the possibility

of the concrete dystopia of a ‘planet of slums’ (Davis, 2006). Free market economies,
especially in the global South, show many (if not all) of the previous features, which
are perceived as market failures (Desai and Loftus, 2013).
The widening socio-economic inequalities have induced the proliferation of a
‘plethora of hidden and alternative economic activities’ as a survival strategy. Those
informal, marginal and diverse economic activities are globally widespread and more
prevalent than capitalistic practices (Gibson-Graham, 2008, 6). They can be highly
experimental, which may involve several failed attempts before succeeding. If so,
the innovation may also have a substantial impact on social, material and environ-
mental well-being if they rise from a socially engaged origin and look to increase the
number of jobs and added value they produce in a solidary and sustainable manner
(Ironmonger, 1996; Schneider and Enste, 2013).
In extreme free market contexts where capitalistic forms of production are the
norm, the apparition of informal economic activities based on an ethos of solidarity
and cooperative principles can become social innovations. If they endure, the
economic activities taking place in a territory expand, hosting not just capitalistic,
but wide and disparate forms of economies (Gibson-Graham et al., 2013). Because of
their potential, those socially innovative economic practices have been studied by the
diverse economy framework, which claims they should be recognised, nourished and
researched, facilitating their consideration as more real and ‘viable objects of policy
and activism’ (Gibson-Graham, 2008, 618).
The diverse economy of cooperatives
A diverse economy serves as an adjective for socio-economic systems with different
kinds of firms, but is also an academic approach for illustrating the immense variety
of capitalistic or non-capitalistic economic activities taking place in a community,
region or nation. Although not without detractors (see Samers, 2005), the framework
characterises an economy according to five variables: labour practices (wage, alterna-
tive paid, unpaid), business enterprises (capitalist, alternative capitalist, non-capitalist),
transactions of goods and services (market, alternative market, open access), property
ownership (private, alternative private, open access), and finance (mainstream market,
alternative market, non-market) (Gibson-Graham, 2008; Gibson-Graham et al., 2013).
Similarly to the concrete utopia (Lefebvre, 1968; Purcell, 2002), the diverse economy
approach is also a ‘performative ontological project’; both encourage attempts to
bring new socio-economic urban landscapes into existence, especially those aiming to
improve the quality of life in collaborative and communal efforts (Gibson-Graham,
2008, 616). When successful, community economies rise as network spaces where inter-
dependent relations are democratically agreed upon by the participating actors and
institutions. Since they can be created at any scale, they could advance the concrete

communal construction of better living socio-economic habitats (Gibson-Graham et
al., 2013; Lefebvre, 1968). However, projects promoting socio-economic autonomy
and community empowerment through experimentation are likely to face repeated
failure. Even though failure can happen for plenty of reasons, if the group keeps
learning and persevering, their resilience will be awarded better chances of success.
The diverse economy approach recognises cooperatives and informal settlements
as examples of non-capitalistic economies, since they are organised around an ethos
of solidarity, have an alternatively paid labour, and distribute their profits between
their members and wider community, according to democratic negotiations between
all the participating actors (Gibson-Graham, 2008; Gibson-Graham et al., 2013;
Roelvink et al., 2015). Building these community economies is crucial for empowering
grassroots movements since they could promote further social and productive innova-
tion processes to transform and achieve social justice, despite starkly unequal power
structures (Muller and Mitlin, 2007; Smith et al., 2014).
Cooperatives have also become essential providers of jobs worldwide. According
to a report made by CICOPA (Eum, 2017), employment in or within the scope of
cooperatives represents at least 279.4 million people across 156 countries, a staggering
9.46 per cent of the total employed population worldwide. Cooperatives are especially
prevalent in some Latin American countries’ history, such as Argentina, Bolivia and
Brazil (Birchall, 1997; 2011; Magni and Günther, 2014); although Chile only has
0.13 per cent of its workforce participating in cooperatives (Unidad de Estudios Minis-
terio de Economía, 2014). They are acknowledged as relevant in dealing with infor-
mality, particularly by the ILO recommendation number 193, which posits that:
Governments should promote the important role of cooperatives in transforming what
are often marginal survival activities (sometimes referred to as the ‘informal economy’)
into legally protected work, fully integrated into mainstream economic life’. (ILO, 2002)
Creating a cooperative might be extremely challenging for the urban displaced,
especially within a neoliberal context. However, by avoiding conventional top-down
state-driven solutions, grassroots-led cooperatives can focus on promoting economic
self-sufficiency, horizontal comradeship and political and civic empowerment
(Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola, 2019). All cooperatives have the political objec-
tive of fostering their informal dwellers’ incorporation in the formal city through the
recognition of rights, instead of just being subjects of charity by states and NGOs.
Grassroots-led cooperatives might then foster ‘new forms of social power emerging
within [a] slum of the Global South’ (Desai and Loftus, 2013, 795), to contest
(dis)empowerment strategies that they face daily, such as their demonisation, margin-
alisation and exclusion (Mitlin and Bartlett, 2018). Yet, this puts them on a collision
course with the actors comfortable with how power is (a)symmetrically distributed in
the urban landscape (McFarlane, 2012; Mitlin, 2008; 2018).

Nonetheless, bringing those economies into existence can be challenging,
especially in informal and stigmatised contexts with few support networks. Therefore,
the diverse economies approach emphasises the decisive role that social actors and
institutions like academia, civil society, grassroots communities and the state should
play in cooperatives’ co-creation and consolidation.
Multi-actor co-production for and with the urban poor
To challenge the negative perceptions of the urban poor and overcome their socio-
economic vulnerability, multi-actor co-production processes have been extremely
useful. Mitlin and Bartlett (2018) summarise that engagement arising from co-produc-
tion provides opportunities to dispute these views by raising the socio-economic
status of low-income residents. Co-production is understood, then, as processes of
material improvement and knowledge as well as capacity and relationship creation.
Through these, social movements take shape, and their agendas are advanced in a
multi-actor effort (Mitlin and Bartlett, 2018). This builds upon the original definition
by Whitaker (1980), where co-production was an explicit political strategy through
which grassroots organisations and the state were able to jointly secure the satisfac-
tion of basic needs while also strengthening civil society’s bargaining position and
direct participation. Hence, a co-production of a cooperative could simultaneously
secure material welfare and promote the partial concretisation of utopic urban
political projects.
Today, co-production might also emerge from other actors, such as NGOs and
academia (Mitlin and Bartlett, 2018), in a process where actors work together horizon-
tally, to overcome longstanding antagonisms and power asymmetries (Simon et al.,
2018), and to advance social and urban justice by challenging the structures that
reproduce inequalities (Castán and Neves, 2018). Hence, it disputes the notion that
research should be done on disadvantaged groups, but with organised citizens instead,
triggering a process of radical change (Mitlin, 2018; Mitlin and Bartlett, 2018).
For a long time, co-production was considered a strategy relevant in the presence
of weak states, unable to provide basic services, guarantee human rights, and/or
decent life conditions (Mitlin, 2008). Although not universally endorsed today (Mitlin,
2018), such a position is still useful for highly neoliberal contexts, where individualism
is the norm. However, co-production efforts cannot be embraced uncritically. Such
processes may be disempowering if: 1) There is a capture of benefits by the local
elite; 2) new and/or complex responsibilities are passed to vulnerable groups (Castán
and Neves, 2018); 3) the social innovation of horizontally organised arrangements
of governance ends up reinforcing market-oriented behaviour, due to a failure in
implementing democratic, participative and horizontally networked interdependen-
cies (Swyngedouw, 2005).

When considering the above, grassroots, community-based cooperatives rising
in extremely free market contexts can be considered a type of socially innovative
approach to supporting self-sufficient economic strategies. This research considers the
experimental multi-actor co-production of the first cooperative bakery in a Chilean
macrocampamento as a project looking to promote a diverse economy and further
social innovations with positive outcomes. Nonetheless, the project also triggered some
contradictions that will be further developed throughout this article and its conclu-
sions. The next section provides a brief discussion of the methods, which will later be
used to describe and evaluate the overall experience.
Methodology
The case of the cooperative bakery is presented following an ‘explanation building’
(Yin, 2009), produced conjointly with key agents involved in the project. The descrip-
tion and analysis of the case study are illustrated from an engaged research stance.
Engaged research implies a rigorous approach to produce a collaborative interaction
with (and not at the cost of) a community. It is aimed at understanding and resolving
issues and challenges of their interest (for them). This entails contesting the assump-
tion that researched subjects are mere ‘vessels that empty themselves into research
indiscriminately’ (Blake, 2007, 414). However, they are considered active participants
in evaluating social endeavours (Blake, 2007).
Engaged researchers worked in a close multi-actor partnership but kept a negotiated
distance of intellectual autonomy by which to analyse the process critically (Ang, 2006).
This way of co-producing knowledge usually implies establishing mutual reciprocity,
long-term relationships, critical assessments of place-based human issues within broader
ecosystems, the embracing of differences and positive conflict to produce knowledge
and empowerment, and the acknowledgment of the ethical/practical repercussions of
methodological decisions (Ang, 2006; Arvanitakis & Hodge, 2012; Magee et al., 2013).
By becoming directly engaged, the researchers were considered to be another key
actor. Thus, they positioned themselves as ethically co-responsible for the successes
and failures of the bakery and directly involved in co-producing useful and critical
knowledge. There were several benefits to being engaged. For instance, there were
plenty of resources available for the researchers to use in their description and under-
standing of the process. The project was further legitimised due to the participation
of universities, the appearance of new opportunities to expand its original aims, and
the fact that local academics got out of their perceived ‘ivory towers’ (Blake, 2007;
Demko, 1988; Readings, 1996). Academic practices also comprised the production
of several publications in local and national newspapers, something Hoffman (2019)
considered to be ‘militant acts’ aimed at actively producing a broader political we
between researchers and researched.

Primary data was gathered between 2017–2019, through observations, notes and
six semi-structured interviews with key actors, representing the macrocampamento,
the bakery (a representative and a cooperativist), the local government, a local NGO,
and academia, all of whom were directly involved throughout the process. The inter-
views took place between January and July of 2019 – around a year after the formation
of the bakery – to better capture a more critical and nuanced evaluation.
Positionality of the researchers
The authors were involved as observant participants, taking notes and collecting
impressions. This engagement requires some clarifications about the positionality of
researchers. Both are male, Chilean and formed on different approaches of critical
theory to social sciences, in which the search for contradictions of social phenomena
is key for producing knowledge. This implied some initial research bias, given that a
more liberal framing of the problem was initially discarded as an analytical framework
for the case. Both researchers lived in Antofagasta when the research was conducted,
with direct involvement in the cultural context where Los Arenales exists. Hence,
their engagement with the community also had political implications, given that this
informal settlement is not transversally accepted by the local community of Antofa-
gasta.
Nevertheless, the methods of analysis and data collection were developed under
a strict code of ethical research framed by one of the funders of the investigation.
Although the positionality of the researchers may have influenced their approach to
the experience, the analysis and conclusions were carried out in a critical fashion,
aiming to provide academic reflections of the process to be shared with a broader
community. Next, the context where the bakery took place is described.
The rising tide of campamentos in Chile and Antofagasta
Chile is exceptionally famous and infamous worldwide, considering its small size.
Among the positive, it has been praised for its economic and political stability, leading
economic growth among OECD and Latin American states (OECD, 2013; World
Bank, 2017). Its principal economic activity is mining, being the world’s biggest
producer of copper, iodine and lithium (USGS, 2018), mostly exploited in the Antofa-
gasta region (see Figure 1) (Arias et al., 2014; Phelps et al., 2015). Infamously, Chile
is the earliest and one of the most vigorous adopters of neoliberal economic policies
worldwide (Harvey, 2005; Klein, 2007), brutally implemented during Pinochet’s dicta-
torship (Constable and Valenzuela, 1993; Salazar and Pinto, 2002; Solimano, 2012),
which fostered economic transformations mostly favouring its wealthiest citizens
(Bohoslavsky et al., 2019).

The widespread perception of Chile as an economic miracle hides the darkest
side of its neoliberalism: extreme inequalities shaped by a concentration of political
power and wealth, and increasing spatial segregation (Boano and Vergara-Perucich,
2017; Phelps et al., 2018). Today, Chile ranks as one of the most unequal nations
among the OECD members (2016). However, the positive international perception
led to an increasing immigration rate during the last decade, paired with a worrying
escalation of racism and xenophobia (INDH, 2017; Tijoux and Díaz, 2014). Simul-
taneously, stagnant wages, relatively slow economic growth, increasing unemploy-
ment and housing costs have exponentially increased the number of families living in
campamentos; especially since 2011, when the commodity super-cycle concluded (see
Figure 2) (CIS TECHO-Chile, 2016; TECHO, 2018).
The Antofagasta region has also been considered an example due to its growth
rates based on extractive industry exploitation (CEPAL, 2009; OCDE, 2009; OECD,
2013). Yet, its celebrated macroeconomic results have failed to produce fair socio-
economic development, especially in its capital city, Antofagasta (Arias et al., 2014;
Phelps et al., 2015). Two crucial challenges troubling the Antofagasta region stand
out:

First, the relatively large international migration received in the last decade
following the copper boom prices, as the immigration rate increased by 327 per cent
between 2005 and 2014 (Departamento de Extranjería y Migración, 2016). Nowadays,
around 11 per cent of the Antofagasta region’s total population are foreign immigrants,
coming largely from Colombia, Peru, and Bolivia (INE, 2017). Second, the increasing
expulsion of part of the local labour force – especially immigrants – into campamentos,
due to increasing housing prices (Contreras et al., 2019; López-Morales et al., 2018;
Stang and Stefoni, 2016; Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola, 2019). The census of 2017
and CASEN 2015 (Encuesta de Caracterización Socioeconómica Nacional [National
Socioeconomic Characterisation Survey]) estimate that around 12 per cent of the
immigrant population in Antofagasta live in an impoverished situation, typically in
campamentos. Between 2007 and 2018 alone there was a rampant 980 per cent increase
in the number of families living in such informal settlements (TECHO, 2018).
The mix of these social issues has caused rising discrimination against the immigrants
and the inhabitants of the campamentos of Antofagasta. Poor immigrants face racism
and xenophobia daily, all of which weaken their own perception as subjects with rights
(Guizardi et al., 2018; INDH, 2017; Stang and Stefoni, 2016; Tijoux, 2016; Tijoux and
Díaz, 2014). Thus, they usually accept informal jobs with extremely low wages and precar-
ious working conditions (Contreras et al., 2019; López-Morales et al., 2018; Stang and
Stefoni, 2016; Stefoni, 2011). All these make developing sustainable ways for generating
incomes and securing dignified living conditions extremely urgent.

Amidst this context, the macrocampamento Los Arenales shows several key features.
First, it is macro since it comprises at least ten different campamentos unified under a
central board of democratically elected representatives named Rompiendo Barreras.
Second, around 80 per cent of its inhabitants are immigrants from other Latin
American countries (GORE, 2016). Third, despite its challenging internal complexity,
size and mixed cultural backgrounds, the organisation has managed to produce a solid
sense of belonging, respect and dignity (Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola, 2019).
Like most of the campamentos in Antofagasta, Los Arenales was constructed
on the foothills of the city (Figure 3), using over 11.4 hectares of public land. The
Chilean state is reluctant to allow the informal occupation of land by low-income
families. It frequently uses the police to repress and evict – in an awfully violent way
– (Handelman, 1975; Salcedo, 2010) or displace those families to ‘temporary camps’
located at the city’s fringe (Contreras et al., 2019). The topology of the terrain is
irregular, arid and lacks facilities for living. Most of the houses are built with light
material, and its internal frontiers are perfectly recognised by the inhabitants of each
settlement. Los Arenales’s geopolitics and land management are based on collective
forces that are organised horizontally in the organisation Los Arenales Rompiendo
Barreras, leading to an extremely dynamic spatial development with new houses built
every month. Despite these difficulties, the resilience, solidarity and will to improve
their living conditions among the inhabitants of Los Arenales have fostered a contin-
uous development of strategic projects such as the cooperative bakery detailed next.

The unlikely making of the first cooperative bakery
In a fortunate series of events, several actors came together in 2017 to participate in
the co-creation of the first cooperative bakery in a Chilean campamento. This was
unusual, considering that only 0.13 per cent of the regional workforce of Antofagasta
participated in a cooperative (Unidad de Estudios Ministerio de Economía, 2014). If
successful, this effort had the potential to become a social innovation and diversify the
local economy, becoming an example of non-capitalistic production. The actors were
the campamento dwellers, the Chilean government, a local NGO and the academia:
1) The most important actors were the inhabitants of the macrocampamento Los
Arenales, politically organised in Los Arenales Rompiendo Barreras.7 Notice-
ably, Chilean shantytowns were powerful left-winged political actors before
the dictatorship; but mass arrests, executions and selective relocations during
that period starkly disempowered them (Handelman, 1975; Rodríguez and
7 In English: Breaking Chains.

### Page 15

14 Martín Arias-Loyola and Francisco Vergara-Perucich
Winchester, 2001). Hence, the recent resurgence of strong political associations
like Rompiendo Barreras is especially relevant.
2) The government participated through the programme Funds for Solidarity
and Social Investments (FOSIS), something novel since the dictatorship estab-
lished a rather distant relationship between informal settlements and the state
(Posner, 2012). FOSIS is part of the Social Development Ministry, and it has a
national budget that is managed and targeted by the local government (Secre-
taría Regional Ministerial de Desarrollo Social). Consequently, FOSIS mostly
avoids funding social investments in informal settlements.
3) Academia was represented by the Culinary School of Universidad Santo Tomás
(UST) and three academics from the Observatorio Regional de Desarrollo
Humano (ORDHUM) – part of the Universidad Católica del Norte (UCN) –
where the authors belong. This was unusual since the highly marketised Chilean
higher education system have a ‘publish or die’ attitude rather than any thought
of working with and for the civil society (Guzmán-Valenzuela and Barnett, 2013;
Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola, 2019).
4) All these actors were coordinated by the local NGO Agrupación de Traba-
jadores del Area Social FRACTAL (ATTAS-FRACTAL),8 well known for its
work with campamentos and immigrants since 2012. Their work was pivotal for this
initiative since they aimed to empower and articulate the campamento’s inhabit-
ants’ long-term political struggle for a radicación and decent living, contrasting
with the usual short-term provision of goods and services done by other NGOs.
The bakery started when FRACTAL undertook a project funded by FOSIS at
the border between Los Arenales and the adjacent formal neighbourhood. The main
goal of this project was to advance towards a more autonomous way of generating
resources to improve the space where they live at, based on the community’s capabili-
ties and learning new skills of self-sufficiency. In 2015, they opened La Casita (the
Little House), a centre for working with children from the neighbourhood through
several activities, especially sports. This became the main hub for laying the founda-
tions of Los Arenales Rompiendo Barreras since, ‘La Casita united the campamento,
[FRACTAL] gathered us and empowered us. [They] gave us a space to gather
and [the] tools […] and networks’ [Los Arenales representative]. That same year,
FRACTAL carried out a series of workshops for the inhabitants of Los Arenales
where they presented several democratic ways of production as, according to them:
The logic of individual entrepreneurship [promoted by the Chilean state] is oriented
to failure. [It just exists to] reduce the poverty indexes instead of focusing on providing
dignified living conditions […] We told [the inhabitants of Los Arenales] that they should
bet for collective entrepreneurship, preferably, cooperatives. (FRACTAL representative)

Several representatives of Los Arenales were paying close attention to those words.
During early 2017, the visit of the UN housing speaker Leilani Farha to Los
Arenales, organised by Rompiendo Barreras and FRACTAL, was pivotal in promoting
the interest of the macrocampamento in considering housing and access to the formal
city as human rights, despite their status as poor foreigners (Vergara-Perucich and
Arias-Loyola, 2019). After her visit, Farha wrote a controversial open letter to Presi-
dent Bachelet, pushing the Chilean state to stop the discrimination towards campa-
mentos by ‘[providing] the necessary legal framework and ensure access to resources
to allow access to different types of tenure beyond ownership, for example cooperative
housing, land trusts and other community ownership models (Farha, 2017, 18–19). The
academics of the ORDHUM met with FRACTAL and Los Arenales Rompiendo
Barreras, after Farha’s visit, to establish a horizontal collaborative alliance between
them and the macrocampamento. Five workshops related to the right of the city were
agreed upon and took place during 2017, where academics stressed that the inhabitants
of campamentos ‘live in a situation of informality, not illegality [and that] fighting for
their housing [also means] fighting for a community’ (bakery representative). In those
workshops, the relevance of thinking about their own utopia was discussed along with
ideas to bring them into existence in a collaborative and feasible manner.
Once the right to the city workshops were finished, the inhabitants of Los Arenales
had already co-produced, with ORDHUM’s academics and FRACTAL, an urban
manifesto and a strategy as their way to bring their concrete utopia into existence
where self-sufficient modes of production were central (Vergara-Perucich and Arias-
Loyola, 2019). The specific interest in forming a cooperative was initially developed by
FRACTAL, since a member socialised his experience of visiting mutual aid coopera-
tives in Argentina. This resonated with several representatives of Los Arenales.
Initially, ‘the idea was to start a work cooperative [to] facilitate the building of our
own houses […], but it was too monstrous, too giant’ (Los Arenales representative).
Nonetheless, that intention was presented to the local FOSIS agency which, despite
being initially reluctant, was convinced of funding a cooperative in Los Arenales
as ‘a space of collective construction’ (FOSIS representative), because of its ‘strong
capacity for self-organisation, [and ability] to articulate itself with several other actors
in a highly autonomous way’ [FOSIS representative].
To get the funds, the National Director of FOSIS was brought to visit Los Arenales,
‘and he [the National Director] almost fainted [because] he told me “this is not the
Chile I know”! [he said he] had not seen any campamentos like that in the country,
especially with that number of different nationalities […] and he said “I will get the
funds, but you have to organise a sustainable project”’ (FOSIS representative).
This led to the fast negotiation and approval of an initial capital of 10.000.000
CLP (around $14,000 USD), provided by FOSIS for starting a food cooperative but
under certain conditions. The whole project had to be finished and running within

six months and FOSIS demanded the signature of an agreement with at least one

institutions were not satisfied with the campamentos [being] in charge [of the project]

A bakery was chosen since bread is highly symbolic in Latin American cultures,9
representing the simple but necessary communal and inalienable struggle to feed the
family, especially when facing stark scarcity (Jelin, 1998). In Los Arenales particu-
larly, bread has cultural meaning as a symbol of sustainable livelihoods and dignity
(Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola, 2019). ‘Here [at Los Arenales] everyone eats
bread. Bread is the first plate on the table’, said a bakery representative, explaining
that bread production was perceived as profitable and relatively easy and cheap
to produce (Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola, 2019). The cooperative logic was
based on ‘[providing] jobs for people without jobs [in Los Arenales], who are mostly
women. But also [the bakery’s] statutes clearly said that 2 per cent of the cooperative
profits must go to improving the [built environment of the] macrocampamento’ (Los
Arenales representative).
Bread was going to be sold at ‘the lowest prices possible […] helping people’s
pockets’ (bakery representative), specifically, 30 per cent cheaper than the average price
in the city. This shows their commitment to achieving sustainable socio-economic goals,
contrasting with the profit-driven strategies of traditional capitalistic businesses, notably
in neoliberal contexts. The bakery also intended to represent and celebrate the multicul-
tural diversity within Los Arenales, offering Chilean, Colombian, Peruvian and Bolivian
bread. Its products were sold based on in-lane sales and delivery throughout the macro-
campamento. Consequently, the inhabitants of Los Arenales were excited, as they felt
the bakery was a way ‘to become a strong community [by] following our own processes
to empower ourselves’ (Los Arenales representative). ‘If this has worked in other places,
why not here?’ was remarked in most of the assembly meetings.
For signing the agreement, Los Arenales was represented by the committee Rayito
de Esperanza (Little Beam of Hope). Together with FRACTAL, FOSIS, ORDHUM
and UST, Los Arenales committed to collaborating in co-producing the bakery.
Rompiendo Barreras initiated the process of making a broad call for cooperativists.
Twenty neighbours from eight committees participated as the initial cooperativist
group, and they agreed to attend the training provided by FRACTAL, UST and
ORDHUM. FRACTAL was hired by the bakery to provide training in coopera-
tivism, technical assistance and support during the whole process, which was filmed
in a documentary freely available on YouTube (CIPNOR, 2018).

Co-producing bread, resilience and empowerment
The actors lacked experience in cooperative ventures and faced a tight deadline,
but recognised the relevance of collective experimentation in bringing ‘a new kind
of economy into existence’ (Gibson-Graham, 2008). Rompiendo Barreras and
FRACTAL oversaw the legalisation process and administered the funds while the
training was planned with ORDHUM and UST. FOSIS limited its participation to
supervising the project, ensuring that administrative and financial aspects were in
order. Once the new government took office, interactions with FOSIS were reduced
to periodic meetings related to achieving goals.
Training began in early 2018, with UST professionals ‘assisting in how to make
bread, as a way to build [our] dream’ (cooperative representative) in forty-five teaching
hours. However, UST professionals carried out the training ‘by lending […] their
equipment […] as quietly as possible […] probably because [Los Arenales] was in
a nebula of illegality. So, to [publicly show their participation] was like they were
validating that model [of informal settlements]’ (FOSIS representative). Hence,
UST’s participation ended with the training, illustrating how practices for developing
self-sufficiency in campamentos are frowned upon in some Chilean universities.
Simultaneously, ORDHUM, from the Economics Department of the UCN,
started training the cooperativists in the basics of how to run a firm, developing
an entrepreneurial attitude and building teamwork, accountability and horizontal
working environments. ORDHUM also participated in the co-production of a
business model canvas with the trainees from Rompiendo Barreras, volunteer students
and FRACTAL and a SWOT analysis presented in Table 2. In parallel, FRACTAL
started training about cooperative firms and values. However, the great number of
workshops – exceedingly condensed into a few weeks – took a heavy toll since, after
a while, the attendance vastly dropped: The fifth workshop organised by ORDHUM

continue providing enough technical assistance to the cooperativists for several reasons,

it was perceived as too close to one of the female leaders. Meanwhile, ORDHUM’s
team was being criticised and beleaguered by some colleagues, local authorities and
NGOs, since the project was considered as a ‘waste of academic time’; and a threat
to more patronising asymmetrical power relationships between the state, other NGOs

academics (Guzmán-Valenzuela and Barnett, 2013), distracted the team.

Conclusions
This article presents the extremely experimental co-produced social innovation
process of creating the first cooperative bakery in a Chilean macrocampamento as a
form of diverse economy for promoting self-sufficiency and urban social justice. This
case is relevant since:
1) the neoliberal Chilean context has not fostered the apparition of cooperatives
nor the funding of projects in informal settlements by the state (Harvey, 2005;
Solimano, 2012);
2) the actors participating in the various stages of the bakery were markedly
disjointed previous to the project;
3) Chilean campamentos have been long-term victims of disempowering practices
by the state since the dictatorship, leading to precarious living conditions
(Handelman, 1975; Salcedo, 2010);
4) institutions working in campamentos usually do not foster self-sufficiency
(Vergara-Perucich and Arias-Loyola, 2019);
5) Chilean universities are focused on scientific production rather than creating
better realities for the disadvantaged (Guzmán-Valenzuela and Barnett, 2013);
6) campamento’s inhabitants and immigrants face strong discrimination, being
increasingly expelled to informal settlements (Contreras et al., 2019; Tijoux and
Díaz, 2014);
7) it provides a critical evaluation of a flawed process, which led to valuable lessons
about the right to experiment and fail, and a higher resilience among the actors

It was highly unlikely that a cooperative bakery run by the most socially vulnerable
people could survive, but it did, providing significant learning for future experiences
in similar contexts.
As the first of its kind, this endeavour involved a considerable amount of trial and
error, where the actors heavily underestimated the issues that could arise. Hence, it
was fundamentally signalling the right to fail, which ensures that the lessons co-produced
will inform better failures in the future. This is especially innovative and relevant when
co-producing social innovations with the urban poor, who do not have the luxury of
continuous experimentation due to their urgent needs. Therefore, public policies must
be implemented to lower the cost of failure, ensuring further experimental (co)produc-
tions of the desired urban and socio-economic utopic imaginaries with and for the urban
displaced. We consider this as one of the main contributions of this experience.
From the co-producing group’s failures in proper training in the basics, lessons
can be drawn regarding cooperative modes of production, running a company
and acknowledging and mediating the internal tensions that may arise in a highly
heterogeneous group. Learning about cooperative modes of production can be more
challenging than understanding how to start a regular business, especially in neolib-
eral contexts, since it implies thinking collectively instead of individually. As a bakery
representative explained: ‘It is good that you teach us the basics […] but it is also
important to keep reinforcing those lessons [because] sometimes you attend a […]
workshop and learn something, but after a few days, you start forgetting. You should
reinforce and add to that knowledge [constantly].’
Thus, workshops should be planned and carried out in a way that the trainees
adequately absorb and apply that new knowledge, instead of just squeezing too much
into too little time. Those supporting the projects with the appropriate know-how, such
as professionals from NGOs, the state and academics, should keep aiding and advising
after finishing the training since at this stage, mistakes and frustration can lead to
increased friction and dropouts. This also means that providing stress relief by helping
with complex responsibilities is imperative, as warned by Castán and Neves (2018).
The conflict exploded when the urgency of securing individual wages for subsistence
clashed with cooperative values based on solidarity, causing a break. This could have
been avoided if cooperative values were better understood and embraced by participant
group, but also with a more active mediation by FRACTAL, ORDHUM and FOSIS.
Securing the participation of people with experience in cooperatives could also provide
grounded experience about the challenges of the whole process, avoiding its idealisation
and unreal expectations. The opportunity cost of the time spent in workshops should
also be reduced, either by holding them after paid working hours (e.g. 7.00 pm) or by
paying the attendants for their time, meaning that proper funding is central.12

However, the horizontal interdependencies implemented between the three main
actors (Swyngedouw, 2005) encouraged mutual trust and allowed for their joint partic-

visible, empowered and admired since, as the most stigmatised inhabitants of Antofa-

project in Chile. The workshops that were conducted in both projects gave rise to
several, and mostly female, new leadership figures within the macrocampamento and
a sense of pride and hope about getting the radicación. Between 2017 and 2019, the
inhabitants of Los Arenales and the bakery’s cooperativists became recognised activ-
ists and travelled abroad for international meetings, training sessions and conferences,
which widened their networks.
Despite its flaws, the participant group – and especially the inhabitants of Los
Arenales – consider the bakery to have improved their quality of life and made them
visible, more respected and more resilient. They are keen to continue their experi-
mentation with horizontal multi-actor and communal forms of organisations to bring
their desired utopia closer to being achieved. This means that the bakery can be
considered a desirable social innovation. Representatives of Los Arenales are now
leading the local campamentos’ struggle for their right to the city, but are also partici-
pating in the feminist and immigrant movements. They have increasing support from
civil society organisations, national and international academics, international activ-
ists and members of parliament. They have even had meetings with the Ministry of
Housing of Chile, about the radicación of campamentos (El Regionalista, 2019). Hence,
they became a beacon for other urban displaced people and a symbol of resilience,
who are also reaching out to universities and NGOs to explore new diverse economies
aimed at their self-sufficiency and emancipation. As a final reflection, a representative

our city] […] these are the proofs [KYC products], these are the ways […] and they
